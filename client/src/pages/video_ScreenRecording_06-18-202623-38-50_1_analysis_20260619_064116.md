The substance in the video is a highly viscous, thick fluid (rosin) being pressed from parchment paper. To recreate this on text, you need to simulate the physics of a thick, sticky liquid succumbing to gravity.

Here is the breakdown of the visual effect:

### 1. Color and Texture
*   **Color:** It is an opaque, creamy, pale golden-yellow (similar to melted butter or thick cake batter).
*   **Texture/Finish:** It has a smooth, slightly glossy surface. It is mostly opaque, but the very thinnest edges might have a tiny bit of translucency. It looks heavy and dense.

### 2. Phase 1: The Emerge (How it starts)
*   **The Origin Point:** In the video, the substance emerges from a straight horizontal line (the crease of the paper). If applying this to text, the origin point would be the bottom edge of the letters.
*   **Initial Shape:** It does not start as individual drips. It begins as a solid, continuous horizontal mass that bulges outward uniformly. It looks like a thick bead of caulk being squeezed out.

### 3. Phase 2: The Formation (How it grows)
*   **Loss of Uniformity:** As the mass grows heavier, the straight bottom edge begins to warp. It forms a wavy, undulating line.
*   **Bulbous Tips:** Gravity pulls unevenly, causing the waves to form distinct "fingers" or drips. The bottom of each drip is rounded and bulbous (thicker), while the part connecting it to the main mass above stretches and becomes slightly narrower.
*   **Multiple Drips:** The video shows three distinct main drips forming from the single horizontal mass.

### 4. Phase 3: The Descent (How it animates)
*   **Viscous Physics:** The animation is not fast like water. It is slow, deliberate, and stretching.
*   **Easing:** The movement starts slowly as the mass builds up, then accelerates slightly as gravity takes over, but it maintains a steady, resistant flow. (In CSS, this would require a custom `cubic-bezier` easing function that starts slow and has a smooth, steady acceleration, rather than a snappy movement).
*   **Stretching:** The drips elongate downwards. The top part remains anchored to the source, while the bulbous tips pull downward, stretching the "neck" of the drip.

### 5. Phase 4: The Merge (The "Gooey" Effect)
*   **Cohesion:** Because the liquid is thick, the individual drips do not break off into separate drops. They remain connected to the main mass at the top.
*   **Merging:** In the video, because the paper is folded into a V-shape, the outer drips slide diagonally inward and merge with the center drip. When they touch, they don't overlap; they seamlessly snap together into a single, thicker stream.

### Summary for CSS Implementation Strategy:
To recreate this on text, you will likely need to utilize the **CSS/SVG "Gooey" filter effect** (`feGaussianBlur` combined with `feColorMatrix` to increase contrast).
1.  Your text would be the anchor point.
2.  You would animate multiple pseudo-elements (the drips) starting from behind the text, scaling downwards (`transform: scaleY()`) or translating downwards.
3.  These pseudo-elements should have rounded bottoms (`border-radius`).
4.  Applying the gooey SVG filter to a container holding both the text and the animated drips will cause the drips to look like they are organically pulling away from the text, creating that stretchy, viscous "neck" and allowing multiple drips to merge smoothly if they get close to each other.
